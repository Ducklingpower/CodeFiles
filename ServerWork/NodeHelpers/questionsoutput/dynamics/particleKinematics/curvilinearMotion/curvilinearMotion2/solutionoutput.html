Level 0

The first step is to break the question down into the x and y components. We can write the equations of motion for the x direction and y direction. Then we can combine them to describe our situation.

Level 1

X component equation of motion:

\( v = \frac{x}{t} \)

Y component equation of motion:

\( y = v_ot+\frac{1}{2}at^2 \) \( \implies \) \( y = -\frac{1}{2}gt^2 \)

Since the ball is changing direction we know that there will be centripetal acceleration (\( \frac{v^2}{r} \)), this tells us that we will need to find the radius at time t = 1.6 seconds.

Try to combine the two equations of motion so that we can use |r| = \( \frac{(1+(\frac{dy}{dx})^2)^{3/2} }{\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} } \) to find the radius.

Level 2

    Combining x and y components:

    \( v = \frac{x}{t} \) \( \implies \) \( t = \frac{x}{v} \)

    \( y = -\frac{1}{2}gt^2 \) plugging in t we get \( y = -\frac{g}{2v^2}x^2 \)

    Now we can use |r| = \( \frac{(1+(\frac{dy}{dx})^2)^{3/2} }{\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} } \), since our equation is a function of x and y.

    Plugging in we get |r| = \( \frac{(1+( -2\frac{g}{2v^2}x)^2)^{3/2} }{-2\frac{g}{2v^2} } \)

    Before we can solve we need to find what x is at t = 1.6 seconds.

Level 3

    To find what x is at t = 1.6 seconds all you have to do is use the x component equation from the beginning.

    x = (v)(t) = (25)(1.6) = 40 meters

    Now we can solve for r, |r| = \( \frac{(1+( -2\frac{9.81}{(2)25^2} (40))^2)^{3/2} }{-2\frac{9.81}{(2)25^2} } = 104.87952575653104 \) meters

    Now we can find tangental acceltation to be \( A_t = \frac{ 25 ^2}{ 104.87952575653104 } = 8.308241381857494 \) \( \frac{m}{s^2} \)

    Now that we have centripetal acceleration you can now compute tangential acceleration.

Level 4

    Since the net acceleration is 9.81 m/s^2, rearrange A = \( \sqrt{A_t^2+A_c^2} \) to solve for tangential acceleration.

    A = \( \sqrt{A_t^2+A_c^2} \) \( \implies \) \( A_t = \sqrt{A^2-A_c^2} \) \( \implies \) \( \sqrt{(9.81)^2-(8.308241381857494)^2} \) = 5.216246269185408 \( \frac{m}{s^2} \)
